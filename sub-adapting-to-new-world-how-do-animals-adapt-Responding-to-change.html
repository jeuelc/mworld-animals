Responding to Change

 
click here

For most of our lives, our cells are dividing all the time. Every time a cell divides, a copy of the DNA is created. But since DNA is billions of nucleotides long, mistakes (or 'mutations') are common in the copying process. Mutations may be random but can be accelerated through environmental factors such as the presence of ultraviolet light or radioactivity. Most of these errors have no major impact, but others are bad and affect the animals' chances of survival.

DNA copying and mutations also occur during reproduction as animals produce their young. Every so often a mutation results in a new genotype that is able to produce a phenotype that is better suited to the environment.

The process whereby animals with negative mutations do not survive is called 'natural selection'. The animals that do survive give birth to the next generation.
