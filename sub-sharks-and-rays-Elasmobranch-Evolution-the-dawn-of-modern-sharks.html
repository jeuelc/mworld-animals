The Dawn of the Modern Sharks

All the sharks we see today came from a second burst of shark evolution around 200 million years ago in the late Triassic to early Jurassic Periods. These sharks became known as the 'neoselacians'. Following the extinction of the dinosaurs, many large marine predators like the ichthyosaurs and plesiosaurs became extinct, freeing up the ocean for sharks to evolve and rule as they do today.

Fortunately for many sailors and seals, one particular 'modern shark', 'Megalodon' (meaning 'big tooth') became extinct about 1.5 million years ago. This was an 18-metre super-predator and is what you could call the great-great-grandfather of the great white and mako sharks.

Today's sharks fall into two groups, the 'galeomorphi' and the 'squalomorphi'. Many of the sharks you may know of, such as the tiger, great white, bronze whaler and whale sharks, belong to the galeomorphi.
